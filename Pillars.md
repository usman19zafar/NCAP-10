How the Three Psychometric Pillars Help NCAP‑10
Even though NCAP‑10 is NOT a human test, these three pillars give us the architecture for building a measurement system.

They give us the blueprint, not the content.

Below is the exact mapping.

1. Factor Analysis (FA)
What it is in human tests:
A method to discover latent abilities (FR, KN, QR, VS, WM).

How it helps NCAP‑10:
FA teaches us how to define clean, non‑overlapping constructs.

NCAP‑10 uses this principle to define its 10 dynamical dimensions:

Stability

Synchrony

Energy Flow

Attractors

Plasticity

Timing

Glial Modulation

Synaptic Favorability

Mutable Memory

Dynamic Reasoning

FA gives us the rule:

Each dimension must measure something unique and not overlap with others.

We borrow the concept, not the math.

2. Item Response Theory (IRT)
What it is in human tests:
A probabilistic model of:

difficulty

discrimination

guessing

information

How it helps NCAP‑10:
IRT teaches us how to build a scoring engine.

NCAP‑10 replaces IRT with dynamical scoring:

difficulty → energy cost

discrimination → stability sensitivity

guessing → noise floor

information → dynamical information gain

IRT gives us the rule:

Tasks must reveal differences between systems, not just produce right/wrong answers.

We borrow the logic, not the equations.

3. Norming + Standardization
What it is in human tests:
A way to compare people across ages using:

standard scores

scaled scores

percentiles

age norms

How it helps NCAP‑10:
NCAP‑10 compares systems, not humans.

So we use architecture‑based norms, not age norms:

system class

hardware configuration

training regime

memory architecture

glial‑field settings

energy budget

version history

Norming gives us the rule:

Scores must be comparable across different systems and configurations.

We borrow the principle, not the tables.
